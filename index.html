Abstract

Learning effective reward functions in Reinforcement Learning (RL) remains a major hurdle, especially in complex robotic tasks. The recent work RL-VLM-F demonstrated that Vision-Language Models (VLMs) could provide high-quality, preference-based reward signals using task descriptions and visual observations. Our project successfully replicates this core finding and introduces two novel extensions to enhance the method's efficiency and flexibility:

  • Tournament-Style Preference Labeling: To improve convergence speed by extracting richer information from each VLM query
  • Multi-Objective RL via Metadata Augmentation: To enable the VLM to handle complex constraints (e.g., task success AND movement efficiency) by incorporating robot metadata (end-effector movement) directly into the preference prompt

We find that the tournament approach is faster than the original method and that metadata augmentation successfully enables the learning of trade-off policies, paving the way for more nuanced VLM-guided robotic learning.

Extension A: Tournament-Style Preference Labeling

The original RL-VLM-F uses a pairwise comparison to determine preferences (Image A vs. Image B). We proposed a tournament-style comparison where the VLM ranks three images simultaneously.

Intuition: By comparing multiple trajectories, the VLM can provide a more informative signal about the quality gradient in the environment, which should accelerate policy convergence. Our experiments confirm this: the tournament approach showed faster learning and achieved comparable or superior success rates, particularly in the MetaWorld Soccer task.

Figure: Learning curves comparing Pairwise vs. Tournament-Style Comparison (Extension A).

Extension B: Multi-Objective RL (MORL)

Robotic tasks often require optimizing for multiple objectives, like success and efficiency. We tackled this by augmenting the VLM input with robot metadata (total end-effector movement) and modifying the objective to favor minimal movement while still completing the task.

Finding: By providing the VLM with quantitative metadata, we demonstrated that it can generate preference labels that reflect a multi-objective trade-off. For the Sweep Into environment, the resulting policy achieved the desired success rate while significantly reducing the total distance the robotic arm moved.

Figure: Modified prompting structure to include metadata, with changes in blue text (Extension B).

Figure: Multi-Objective RL (MORL) results. The green line shows a trade-off policy: similar success with lower total movement (Extension B).

Conclusion & Future Work

  • Replication Success: We successfully replicated the core RL-VLM-F method.
  • Accelerated Learning: Tournament-style comparisons prove to be a viable strategy for faster policy convergence.
  • Expanded Capability: The VLM's ability to incorporate external, non-visual metadata is crucial for scaling RL-VLM-F to complex, multi-constrained real-world tasks.
  • Future Work: We will continue exploring the optimal size for the tournament comparison, investigating the use of temporal (video) data instead of single snapshots, and applying the metadata augmentation technique to more complex tasks with real-world physical constraints.
